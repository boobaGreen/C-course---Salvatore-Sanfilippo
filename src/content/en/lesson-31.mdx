## The New Role of the Programmer

The arrival of _Large Language Models (LLMs)_ and coding agents has radically changed our profession. We no longer have to struggle for five or ten minutes writing boilerplate code; we can delegate the writing process and focus on **architecture** and **correctness**.

<Callout type="info" title="The Importance of Fundamentals">
  Why then have we learned raw C so far? Because to guide and *audit* (verify)
  an AI agent, you must perfectly understand memory, pointers, and machine
  limits. The AI writes the code, but **you** are responsible for the result.
</Callout>

## Optimization: From Global to Local

Let's return to the program from the last lesson. The problem was slowness: every time we randomly changed 3 bytes, the algorithm recalculated the difference over the **entire image** (49,152 pixels x 3 colors). Ridiculous!

Salvatore asks the AI to change the logic: instead of calculating the global difference, we work on a single **8x8 block at a time**.

### Complexity Evolution

By moving from total to local recalculation, we've transformed a heavy algorithm into a series of instantaneous micro-operations.

## The Art of Prompting and Dithering

Salvatore notices that restricting updates to isolated blocks creates "blocky artifacts" (similar to JPEG compression) because it ignores the edges.

A true **technical conversation** begins with the AI:

1. Asks to work only on local blocks.
2. Annotates the AI's errors (e.g., the loss of the _dithering_ effect).
3. Proposes a solution: update colors by choosing coordinates **unaligned** with the physical 8x8 blocks, allowing overlaps.
4. Corrects the AI regarding the fact that the 2x2 'kernel size' for _dithering_ had been lost.

<Callout type="pro" title="Dithering with a 2x2 Kernel">
  *Dithering* is the art of placing pixels of two colors next to each other to
  imitate a third. By calculating the difference (error) considering groups of
  2x2 pixels at a time (a kernel), Simulated Annealing understands when two
  adjacent pixels create a good gradient.
</Callout>
